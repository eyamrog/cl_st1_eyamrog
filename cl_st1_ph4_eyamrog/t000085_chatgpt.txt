The Four-Color Theorem originated from the challenge of coloring countries on a map, remaining unsolved since 1853 for over 170 years. By the end of the 20th century, computational tools were utilized to solve this problem, yet an algebraic proof remains elusive. This article provides a concise overview of the original problem of coloring maps on a spherical surface. The process involves converting a spherical map into a planar map using polar coordinates, describing country borders as real implicit equations, and transitioning them from real space to complex space. Within the complex space, rules for coloring maps are articulated as a system of algebraic equations and inequalities. An illustrative map is colored, accompanied by an explanation of the solvability of these systems. Building upon this example, a general theory for coloring maps is developed. Since all transformations employed are reversible, the solution for the planar map can be inverted to solve the spherical map. These operations primarily entail straightforward algebraic manipulations and incorporate some fundamental calculus concepts.
