16 Abstract 17 Taxonomy is essential to biological sciences and the priority field to be supported in face of the biodiversity 18 crisis. The industry of scientific publications has made extensive use of bibliometric indexes, resulting in 19 distortions to institutions, organizations, and researchers, such as the side effect known as Journal Impact 20 Factor (JIF) mania. Inadequacies of the most widely used bibliometric indexes from giant companies 21 Clarivate™ (InCites™) and RELX™ Elsevier B.V. (Scopus®) to assessment of the relevance of taxonomic 22 publications were considered as one of the impediments for the progress of this field. Recently, Clarivate 23 suppressed the mega-journal Zootaxa, focused on taxonomy, from Journal Citation Reports (JCR), a 24 database with 12,000 periodicals. Zootaxa suppression, together with other 32 journals, was based on an 25 unusual high proportion of self-citations. Suppressed journals would thus not receive a value of JIF for 26 2020. A prompt reaction from the scientific community against the suppression of Zootaxa took place and, 27 accordingly, Clarivate announced its reinstatement. This situation exposed many persistent myths and 28 misuses of bibliometric indexes. The goal of this study is to shed light on the impacts of bibliometric indexes 29 to the taxonomic field and on underlying aspects of the suppression of Zootaxa. Our major question is 30 whether the suppression of any journal from JIF can really affect the production in the taxonomic field. We 31 explored data metrics from the JCR (Web of Science Core Collection™) for 2010–2018 of the top ten 32 zoological journals (eight are included in JCR) in the number of new taxa and journals focused on or 33 regularly publishing taxonomic studies, totaling 123 journals. Zootaxa shows higher levels of self-citations 34 than similar journals. We consider that two possible explanations provided for the high number of self- 35 citations, i.e., Zootaxa’s scope on taxonomy and the fact that it is a mega-journal, are inadequate. Instead, 36 putative explanations are related to the “Zootaxa phenomenon,” a sociological bias that includes visibility, 37 2 and potential harmful myths that portray Zootaxa as the unique journal that publishes taxonomic studies 38 with an inviting JIF value. Menaces to taxonomy as a science come from many sources and the low 39 bibliometric values of its journals are only one of the factors that contribute for establishing the so-called 40 taxonomic impediment. We suggest rejection of bibliometric indexes, including JIF, instead of considering 41 them when convenient. Taxonomists as a community, instead of being deeply focused on journal metrics 42 endorsing the villainy of bibliometric policies imposed by dominant companies, should be engaged with 43 renewed strength in actions directly connected to the development and promotion of this science. 44

47 Introduction 48 Every middle of year giant companies on scientific data analytics, the American-British Clarivate™ (InCites™) 49 and the Dutch RELX™ Elsevier B.V. (Scopus®), release their metrics for scientific journals indexed in their 50 huge databases, among them the Journal Impact Factor™ (JIF) and the CiteScore™, respectively. These 51 metrics have been adopted as major qualifiers by several countries as a single measure of the quality of the 52 produced research in their universities and institutes. Generally, funding for research in these institutions is 53 derived from the taxes paid by the citizens of a given country. This policy produces a sort of quest or JIF 54 mania for publishing in higher-ranked journals (Ioannidis & Thombs 2019). Therefore, depending on the 55 impact factor, a researcher has better chances of evolving in his/her career, earn prestige, win grants, etc. 56 Thus, these metrics have a strong impact on how and what scientific investigation can currently be 57 conducted. 58 On the last day of June of this year, an interruption of the colossal concerns about the Covid-19 59 pandemic affected taxonomists around the world. An issue break through the media due to the 60 involvement of zoologists from many countries: the suppression of the mega-journal Zootaxa, a periodical 61 focused on zoological taxonomy, from the Journal Citation Reports™ (JCR) Science Edition metrics by 62 Clarivate. Based on a high proportion of self-citations, along with other 32 journals from the 12,000 in JCR 63 database, Zootaxa would not receive a value of Journal Impact Factor (JIF) for 2019; however, it would keep 64 the values for previous years and still be indexed on the Clarivate Analytics platform. 65 By this time, with the publication of JIF 2019 by JCR, which called the attention of editors and 66 authors who were eager to see how journals were ranked, passionate discussions arose because of 67 Zootaxa’s suppression. A prompt reaction, hardly seen before, through many letters of support to Zootaxa 68 and petitions from several societies and researchers, forced Clarivate to review its decision. We believe that 69 suppression of Zootaxa entails so many unique elements that it needs a closer inspection. Some supporting 70 letters could actually be considered political manifestos and others were very naïve, not to say alarmist or 71 simply inaccurate in interpreting the fact as a new attack to taxonomy as a science. Among the utterly 72 passionate arguments was the one that Zootaxa is the single vehicle to publish taxonomic papers 73 3 nowadays, a statement obviously far away from the truth. At the end of July, in a short statement on 74 Twitter, Clarivate announced that Zootaxa and the International Journal of Systematic and Evolutionary 75 Microbiology would be reconsidered in the regular refresh of the JCR to be published in September. Cases 76 of suppression are common and not unique to the Clarivate platform, being most of them due to 77 accusations of artificial boost or inflation of impact factors (e.g., Cortegiani et al. 2020). A particular case, 78 almost a decade ago, had a wide repercussion among researchers when four journals edited in Brazil were 79 suppressed under accusation of a citation-stacking scheme, a sort of cartel in which self-citations are 80 exchanged among a group of journals (Van Norden 2013). Noticeably, cases of suppression in the past 81 hardly received any sympathy from the scientific community, except from people directly involved as 82 editors, perhaps as a signal that sectors of the academic community agreed with the suppression and 83 considered that the affected journals deserved such “punishment.” Once discarded from JIF, a journal is 84 excluded from the gold rush of academia targeting high-impact outlets. 85 In a system full of anachronisms, in which traditional journals supported by museums or scientific 86 societies are struggling to survive and the scientific publishing industry is led by giant publishers such as 87 John Wiley & Sons, Elsevier, and Springer Nature, among others with profit margins comparable to those of 88 major players in drug, bank, and auto companies (Larivière et al. 2015), it is at least curious to perceive the 89 commotion around the suppression of Zootaxa. We became intrigued and thus decided to provide some 90 reflections aiming to shed light on underlying aspects of this issue. We believe that many of the arguments 91 that were given in the supporting letters are based on misunderstandings about these metrics or are biased 92 by personal interests due to the pressure to publish in high-impact journals. In addition, some points are 93 also potentially misplaced. Bibliometric data are plagued by myths and misunderstandings (Glänzel 2008). 94 Our goal is to discuss the following questions. Can the suppression of any journal from JIF really 95 affect the production in the taxonomic field? What are the consequences of Zootaxa suppression to 96 taxonomy as a science? What means self-citation? What is the average JIF for taxonomic journals? Is 97 Zootaxa a victim of its success? So, what is actually going on? To properly address these aspects, we first 98 need to clarify a few concepts and dig further into the current situation of taxonomic journals, impact 99 measures of scientific publications, and the role of individuals and mega-corporations in this arena. 100
101 Material and methods 102 We explored citation data including JIF, most-cited journals, and self-citation metrics from the Journal 103 Citation Reports (Web of Science Core Collection™) of the last nine years (2010–2018) of the top ten 104 zoological journals (TTJ, eight are included in JCR) when the number of new available names (based on the 105 last five years of ION/Zoological RecordsTM – ZR) is considered. We also checked up for journals focused on 106 or regularly publishing taxonomic papers included in the Zoology and Entomology categories. Data from 107 Zootaxa were retrieved from JCR 2018 because this journal was suppressed from the current edition and 108 will only reappear in September. Journals included in both Zoology and Entomology categories were 109 4 considered simply as Zoology. Among 168 journals in Zoology and 101 in Entomology, 73 and 48 (both 110 numbers include “plus” Zootaxa) were selected. Data of a total of 123 journals were compiled. In order to 111 analyze the selected journals with available data from 2010–2018, a descriptive statistics approach 112 including arithmetic mean of the bibliometric variables, their standard deviations, and the ratio between JIF 113 without self-citations and JIF was used to investigate the influence of self-citations on JIF. This approach 114 was conducted among the top ten journals (TTJ) in ZR and those in Zoology and Entomology categories. The 115 percentage of self-citations and the ratio between JIF without self-citations and JIF per year of the journals 116 between quartiles 2 and 3 (Q2 and Q3) in their categories plus TTJ were analyzed with non-parametric one- 117 way analysis of variance Kruskal–Wallis H test (Dalgaard 2008) in R software (R Core Team 2018). Thus, 118 journals with similar scope and JIF to Zootaxa were considered. In practice, all journals publishing 119 taxonomic papers with JIF 2019 ranging from 0.25–2.315 in the categories Zoology and Entomology were 120 included. This last criterion can be considered arbitrary because the proportion of taxonomic papers is 121 dissimilar among the journals. Clearly, Zootaxa and ZooKeys, for example, have a greater amount of 122 taxonomic papers, even when the fact that these journals accept studies on different subjects of biological 123 sciences is considered. However, for the purposes of our discussion it is reasonable to consider such 124 journals as similar in scope. The comparison is not easy because the original scope of Zootaxa is unique, 125 due to its intent of “rapid publication of high-quality papers on any aspect of systematic zoology” and its 126 focus on long papers. However, Zootaxa publishes today virtually any subject associated to zoological 127 taxonomy/systematics, including biographies and points of view on theoretical subjects. Therefore, it is fair 128 to conclude that all kinds of zoological papers are published in that journal, except those essentially dealing 129 with ecological or experimental issues. The list of the journals and selected metrics are available in Table 1 130 and Supplementary File 1. Note that the categorization of journals follows Web of Science’s rules, so that 131 many taxonomic journals that publish studies in the areas of zoology or entomology have not been 132 included because they are listed in any other of 178 categories in the Science Edition database. 133

171 Discussion 172 What are journal-level metrics? 173 As non-bibliometric researchers, we suppose that measures in bibliometric science were created with some 174 genuine purposes, which entail goals other than supposedly assessing the quality of research or 175 researchers. Originally these indexes aimed to be objective tools for helping librarians in the development 176 of journal collections (Haustein & Larivière 2015). According to Keith Collier (Senior Vice President of 177 Product, Science Group Clarivate, https://bit.ly/31gOMg3), the JIF mission is “to provide a thorough, 178 publisher-neutral, multifaceted view of journal performance, reflecting the world’s highest-quality scientific 179 and scholarly literature.” The hope relies on the citation frequency that would reflect a journal value and 180 the use made of it and shows the average citations per published paper in a given journal (Garfield 1972). 181 6 Apart from the controversies of whether JIF actually assesses a journal “quality,” it aims, together 182 with other bibliometric indexes, the recognition of patterns and trends in publications. Since its creation in 183 the beginning of the 1970 decade (Garfield 1972, however, mentioned that it was designed in 1955; see 184 also Garfield 2006), the metric became strongly popular and has been adopted as a major parameter for 185 evaluating the quality of research, a topic certainly controversial (Hecht et al. 1998, Alberts 2013). The 186 index is a very simple measure calculated from the ratio between the number of citations along a year 187 (numerator) and number of papers published along the two previous years (denominator) — i.e., JIF 2019 is 188 the number of citations in 2019 from papers published in 2017 and 2018 divided by the number of 189 published papers in 2017 and 2018— (Garfield 2006). So, in a certain way, it shows how trendy papers or 190 subjects published by a journal are, as well as if they are achieving a wide audience. The bad twist occurred 191 when organizations, including governmental funding agencies, reached the conclusion that, since journals 192 are evaluated by their impact, bingo, the scientific production in universities, institutes, and graduate 193 courses, as well as the researchers themselves, should be evaluated in the same manner. However, there is 194 a flawed logic in extrapolating indexes such as JIF to evaluate work and careers. Hence, the JIF is recognized 195 without doubt as being the most widely misused and abused bibliometric index in academic science (Hecht 196 et al. 1998, Haustein & Larivière 2015, Ioannidis & Thombs 2019). 197 The adoption of scientific bibliometric indexes such as JIF has grown, especially in the last two 198 decades, as a way to objectively evaluate the strongly competitive field of academic careers. However, 199 there are many studies showing perverse pitfalls, for both researchers and organizations, of this use and 200 interpretation of JIF (e.g., Hecht et al. 1998, Alberts 2013, Chapman et al. 2019). The quest and struggle for 201 publishing in high impact journals produced the JIF mania (Ioannidis & Thombs 2019). 202 Although the use of JIF is not recommended for ranking individuals (Alberts 2013), its impact in the 203 real-life academic career is crystal clear. It is widely perceived that an academic researcher can only evolve 204 in his/her career by means of publishing in journals with high JIF values. The metric has well-known 205 limitations when used to evaluate both journals or individual papers, because the index is strongly sensible 206 to what is considered a citable item (The PLoS Medicine Editors 2006); also, it is characterized by a misuse 207 of statistics such as media and median (Vanclay 2012) and may be radically influenced by a single or few 208 papers (e.g., Dimitrov 2010). Its widespread adoption leads to several distortions such as unjustified multi- 209 authored papers and schemes by journals to artificially increase JIF or impact inflation; these schemes are 210 among the most common outcomes of the JIF mania. Because of the metrics inflation, the bibliometric 211 platforms act as judges to prevent these types of distortion, excluding or punishing “deviant” journals. 212 Indexing platforms such as Clarivate/JCR, for instance, adopt no less than 24 criteria into a putatively 213 unreproducible method of analysis. When a journal disagrees or does not fulfill one of these criteria, it is 214 suppressed (Clarivate 2020a). The lack of transparency greatly affects our ability to properly evaluate 215 journal suppressions. The philosophical dilemma “Who watches the watchmen?”, eternalized in the famous 216 graphic novel Watchmen, written by Alan Moore, fits well here. 217 7
218 Metrics and Taxonomy 219 Undeniably, exploring biodiversity is a core issue for the entire biological sciences (and humanity). In this 220 context, taxonomic research is an essential priority in face of the current biodiversity crisis (Wheeler et al. 221 2012). The concept of taxonomy in biological sciences has a wide range of meanings, varying from the 222 reductionist, atomized, and merely descriptive harmful view known as alpha taxonomy (e.g., Mayr 1969) — 223 largely denoted as a minor science, old fashioned and intellectually poor — to a wide sense of taxonomy as 224 the biggest among all biological sciences (e.g., Wheeler 2008), equivalent to the whole field of comparative 225 biology. This wider view, which is adopted here, embraces from primary data acquisition in field 226 expeditions to morphological, genomic, and even ecosystem analyses. Thus, it considers taxonomy as a 227 relevant hypothesis‐driven science. However, ordinary taxonomic research executed day-by-day is a 228 generally low-cost activity that employs few technological tools. It is focused on the study of natural history 229 collections with the goal of characterizing and making available basic data on biological entities. This work 230 often involves the study of the morphology of poorly known taxa, an unknown sex of a given species or 231 developmental stages, as well as undescribed taxa. Taxonomists must frequently work with poorly known 232 subjects, looking for the novelty, odd, and thus dealing with unpopular or even neglected topics. Therefore, 233 there are many cases of fine, well-written and beautifully fully illustrated comprehensive taxonomic 234 monographs on animal groups that will probably rarely be cited. The small number of citations might even 235 be related to the fact that such monographs successfully solve most of the basic taxonomic questions 236 affecting one taxon. One colorful example was mentioned during the Brazilian Congress of Zoology in 2014. 237 A colleague entomologist raised a simple question: “I am studying one of the smallest orders of insects 238 (Zoraptera, the angel insects) with no more than four dozens of extant species, so what is the chance of 239 citation, within only two years, of a paper that provides a great contribution on this group, including the 240 description of new species?” Problems with the low rate of citations in taxonomy are widely discussed and 241 the inadequacy of JIF for basic sciences is often mentioned (e.g., Krell 2000, Rafael et al. 2009). This is a 242 paradox caused by the fact that taxonomy must, in part, necessarily deal with basic descriptive subjects, 243 the new and unexpected, focusing on small parts of the tree of life. 244 Taxonomy is known as a science in crisis affected by losses of positions in institutions and reduction 245 of funding resources. In addition to this scenario of gradual loss of workforce and grants, the discipline is 246 also damaged by the biases or inadequacies of these so-called indexes of “quality” (see Ebach et al. 2011). 247 Some solutions for low citations suggested the mandatory citation of references in which authorities 248 erected new taxa (original descriptions) whenever a name was mentioned in a study, a rule endorsed by 249 Zootaxa but not strictly enforced by the journal. This rule would partially explain its high level of self- 250 citations. However, this strategy is deeply misleading, because original descriptions, especially the old ones, 251 are often not adequate for species characterization and recognition. A more straightforward approach 252 would be to make clear which concept of species is being adopted and provide the bibliographic source 253 8 (see Meier 2017). Another important point is that multidisciplinarity in biological sciences has blurred the 254 limits among traditional disciplines, even the descriptive ones. All these aspects were suggested as 255 reasonable explanations for the high levels of Zootaxa self-citations. However, they are not valid because 256 there are many other journals currently accepting taxonomic studies, being either purely descriptive or 257 including broader analytical approaches; these journals are obviously attractive in the context of the JIF 258 mania game (Supplementary File 1). 259
260 Zootaxa phenomenon and suppression quarrel 261 As authors of papers on distinct zoological taxa, editors of special issues, and reviewers of manuscripts 262 submitted to Zootaxa along the last ten years, we feel comfortable to offer an opinion on the journal and 263 its impact in the taxonomic world, an actual phenomenon that transformed it into the leading vehicle for 264 making new zoological names available. 265 Since its establishment, Zootaxa has become a prestigious forum for promotion and discussion of 266 all topics of taxonomic science and thus reached a distinguished position among other similar journals. 267 Unquestionably, the birth of the journal was a milestone to the field of zoological taxonomy. Started in 268 2001 with a hybrid platform of publication (i.e., the payment of Article Processing Charges – APC by authors 269 is optional for making the paper Open Access – OA), when 300 pages were published, the journal increased 270 to 32,330 pages in 2010 (Zhang 2011) and ended 2019 with the impressive record of 47,528 pages; the 271 latter comprising 2,400 papers in 176 volumes (data compiled from Zootaxa’s site). In its first decade, 272 Zootaxa has made available about 20–25% of the new nomina per year (Zhang 2014). In the last five years, 273 it has become the main journal, truly the leader in the field of descriptive taxonomy, with 24,722 (26.57% 274 of the total) newly erected taxa made available (ION/Zoological RecordsTM 2020). Despite its few years of 275 existence, the journal has received remarkable status and visibility. Papers published in it have potentially 276 higher chances of being cited by fellow taxonomists, unlike the situation in many other similar journals in 277 the field that clearly have a lower visibility. Zootaxa has been the first choice for a legion of young 278 taxonomists for their very first papers. The relatively high JIF of the journal is certainly among the reasons 279 for this choice. Furthermore, for those zoologists who are not primarily taxonomists but who eventually 280 decide to publish a taxonomic paper, the journal is also probably the first choice, if not the single one 281 known. Indeed, Zootaxa is so influential nowadays that a somewhat pejorative term, “Zootaxa author,” has 282 been coined, meaning those researchers who only publish in the journal or have a massive amount of their 283 papers in it, reaching 80% or more. Why this phenomenon? Why does a journal congregate such a huge 284 parcel of publications in a field? Is this situation actually good for taxonomy? 285 For almost a decade Zootaxa was the single big (or mega) journal in the field designed to attend 286 taxonomic science, even though several smaller journals also published most of their issues with a high 287 amount of taxonomic papers. Today Zootaxa has competitors with the advantage of having either Gold 288 Open Access (GOA) or Diamond Open Access (DOA) policies, such as the European Journal of Taxonomy 289 9 (first issue published in 2011) and ZooKeys (first issue in 2008). However, in the case of ZooKeys, a 290 minimum APC of €700 is required for mandatory open access; this is a huge obstacle, especially for 291 researchers from developing countries, outside the group of those countries considered of lowest income, 292 who do not automatically qualify for a fee waiver. The Zootaxa initiative from Magnolia Press Ltd. was so 293 successful that it stimulated the creation of some new journals, including Phytotaxa, its sibling version 294 dedicated to plant sciences. Data on Magnolia Press, which is based in New Zealand, is not easy to obtain. 295 In the company’s website (magnoliapress.com) not much information is given, for instance, which is the 296 registered business model (for-profit or not-for-profit). 297 The great significance of Zootaxa cannot be denied and it has become the most important vehicle 298 for the publication of taxonomic studies. However, it is obviously not the single journal devoted to 299 taxonomic science, such as depicted by some of the supporting letters. So, why has the suppression caused 300 that enormous commotion? A quick answer is because in some megadiverse countries, such as Brazil in 301 which most of the fauna remains undescribed, the higher education and scientific organizations evaluation 302 systems have entirely embraced bibliometric indexes (e.g., Curry 2018, Krüger 2020, Reategui et al. 2020). 303 Therefore, these metrics play an important role in the system and, for instance, a Brazil-based author’s 304 choice of a scientific journal is largely based on values such as JIF. Consequently, the suppression of Zootaxa 305 was received as a serious setback for taxonomists in such countries, especially so of course for those who 306 publish most or even all their papers in the journal. This last aspect has a clear influence on the high rate of 307 self-citations, as well as on the JIF of Zootaxa (Figures 1–3, 5), even considering that Clarivate recognized 308 that 20% of papers on zoology were published by the journal. 309
310 What is self-citation and its consequences? 311 An important distinction should be made between two categories of self-citation, individual (author) and 312 collective (journal) self-citation, although both potentially result in a boost of bibliometric indexes. There 313 are many legitimate reasons for a researcher to cite his/her earlier works; in many cases, self-citations are 314 unavoidable, depending on the circumstances or subject (Glänzel 2008). For example, an author could have 315 been the single authority on a taxon during the last thirty years or present a high production in a 316 specialized field. In these situations, self-citation alone is not necessarily fraudulent. Concerns arise when 317 similar citations are not received in the work of other researchers in the field (Haustein & Larivière 2015) 318 or, more commonly, based on the myth that self-citations help to artificially increase one's own position in 319 the community (Glänzel 2008). Differently, collective (journal) self-citation would be more problematic and 320 is most probably a side-effect of the JIF mania, caused by the competition among journals for higher journal 321 ranking, prestige, and higher monetary earnings through higher subscription pricing, which is often 322 connected to journal-level bibliometrics. Dear readers do not be naïve: academic publications are million 323 dollars businesses, truly having high profit margins (Monbiot 2011, Larivière et al. 2015). It is thus not 324 surprising that journals engage in “impact factor wars” to manipulate their metrics using strategies such as 325 10 citation stacking, enlargement of cited references during the review process to include papers from the 326 own journal (sometimes even coercive self-citation), and rejection of studies with low potential of citation 327 (Haustein & Larivière 2015). Thus, a high level of self-citations in a journal is not easy to understand and 328 should be checked with caution. 329 Self-citation phenomena, either of author or journal types, have been deeply investigated from 330 various perspectives, including sociological and bibliometric aspects. A review focused on author self- 331 citation and all its technical nuances was presented by Szomszor et al. (2020). Generally, high levels of self- 332 citation are condemned, particularly when journal self-citation is interpreted as the result of manipulation 333 for boosting indexes; in these situations, it has of course been determined that the biased metrics should 334 not be considered for analyses of influence or impact (Ioannidis & Thombs 2019). However, self-citation 335 can be legitimate in certain circumstances (Chorus & Waltman 2016). Consequently, levels of self-citation 336 are not easy to analyze. Ioannidis & Thombs (2019) argued that these levels naturally vary, and high levels 337 may be justifiable in highly specialized journals or in disciplines with few available journals. 338 Zootaxa hardly meets the aforementioned criteria for reasonable justification of high self-citations. 339 Also, self-citation has increased in the journal over the years (Figure 4). Zootaxa is clearly not highly 340 specialized. A quick examination of its issues will confirm this point and taxonomy as a whole is far from 341 having only a few other available journals, at least to most groups. We compiled 123 journals that publish 342 taxonomic papers, solely in the JCR database (Table 1). Therefore, there are clearly many options since 343 these journals surely publish a great deal of descriptive taxonomy (Figures 1–2). If specialization were true 344 for Zootaxa, we would expect that more specialized journals devoted to small groups, such as 345 Odonatologica (dragonflies) and Acarologia (mites), which together represent a small part of extant 346 diversity, would present similar or even higher self-citation levels, which is not the case (Figures 1–2, 5). On 347 the other hand, we would also expect that journals specialized in megadiverse groups, such as beetles, 348 bees, moths, butterflies, etc., would likewise have high levels of self-citation, which again is not the case 349 (Figures 2, 5). Even journals dealing with taxa from a specific region of the world, such as Neotropical 350 Ichthyology or South American Journal of Herpetology, also present significantly lower levels of self-citation. 351 Therefore, the scope of Zootaxa, with its focus on taxonomy, does not explain the high level of self- 352 citations. Instead, an explanation should be looked for in the elements of the Zootaxa phenomenon 353 depicted above. A relevant aspect to be observed in this discussion is that the great majority of the 354 citations given to the analyzed journals came from Zootaxa (Figures 1–3). 355 In addition, Chorus & Waltman (2016) carefully studied journal self-citation and proposed a 356 measure to evaluate boosts in the JIF, detecting disproportional and potentially unethical behavior 357 (“Impact Factor Biased Self-Citation Practices”). They did not consider their measure unfailing and 358 discussed a few cases when self-citation would be legitimated. The latter include distinct situations. For 359 instance, a researcher could be inspired by recent studies published in a journal and thus decides to 360 conduct similar research; accordingly, that journal would naturally be an important source and his/her first 361 11 choice for publication. Also, there are situations when, after finishing a manuscript, an author realizes that 362 most of the cited references are from a given journal; the latter becomes again a naturally expected option. 363 We believe that such cases are strongly associated to the Zootaxa mega-journal phenomenon and appear 364 to partially explain its high levels of self-citation. 365 We are confident that a journal can publish high-quality, robust science regardless of its level of 366 self-citation. There is not necessarily any relationship between the rate of journal-level self-citation and the 367 quality of the research published in a journal, particularly in the case of high output journals such as 368 Zootaxa. Clarivate appears to want to promote a sense of competition among journals, so that it can sell its 369 journal ranking data and analytics – clearly, zoological taxonomists and their publishing and citing behaviors 370 do not fit the model that Clarivate seemingly wants to promote. Who is wrong here? The community of 371 scientists producing taxonomic science for which they were specifically trained to, or the profit-driven 372 analytics company that appears to know nothing about taxonomy and yet still wants to rank and 373 supposedly provide sound judgement on the quality of taxonomic journals? We think of course that the 374 scientific community knows best, whereas Clarivate appears to know or care very little about robust 375 science. 376
377 Is the suppression a new attack on taxonomy? 378 Based on the Zootaxa suppression and the academic engagement into a bandwagon sympathetic 379 commotion, opinions in social media, and letters from societies and researchers (e.g., SBH 2020, SOL 2020, 380 Van Damme 2020), mainly from megadiverse countries, which appear to be in favor of the journal and ask 381 Clarivate to review its decision, two main conclusions could be unearthed: (1) JIF is very important to 382 taxonomy and (2) taxonomy is under attack. We seriously doubt both conclusions and invite the reader to 383 carefully consider these aspects. 384 Why do researchers choose to publish in Zootaxa? Several reasons influence the preference of a 385 researcher for a specific journal. Certainly, scope, visibility, prestige in the field, and JIF are among the most 386 influential criteria. It is realistic to assume that most of the authors of Zootaxa are looking for a journal that 387 has fast reviewing and production processes, is free of charge to authors (no APC), has a comparatively high 388 JIF, and has no limit of pages for a manuscript. Authors and readers of the journal seem not to be 389 concerned about the hybrid policy of paywall for accessing most of the published issues, with few published 390 articles having GOA through payment by authors (APC-OA). Among the reasons for this complaisance are 391 the article-processing charges for most open access journals with values of hundreds of Euros or US Dollars, 392 generally excessively expensive for researchers from developing countries, the possible economic situation 393 of most contributors (e.g., Brazilian researchers are authors of most papers in the journal, 394 https://bit.ly/2Y0hSQ9), and the open access is viable through platforms of self-archiving, such as 395 ResearchGate, or websites, such as Sci-Hub; the latter illegally makes paywalled content available for free 396 and is regarded as “piracy.” High APC costs are clearly impeditive for researchers from most countries and 397 12 for small research groups lacking big budgets. Also, there are certainly many other priorities for spending 398 limited research money. Nevertheless, open access through platforms such as Sci-Hub is deprived of 399 respect for the intellectual property or copyright laws and certainly raises many moral issues. Therefore, it 400 is at least controversial that authors are opposed to paying fees to APC-GOA journals and are in favor of 401 hybrid platforms because it is possible to break paywalls to access payment-based content. 402 The holy grail quest for Diamond open access (no APC for authors, DOA) versus paywall policies 403 creates a paradox: how can journals cover the costs involved in publishing, copyediting, DOI generation, 404 data insertion into biodiversity databases, file archiving, etc.? These controversies concerning OA were 405 depicted with vibrant colors during the gradual transition of big publishers’ journals, such as Diversity and 406 Distributions, from readers' payment to authors' payment in an APC-OA model (Peterson et al. 2019). 407 Gradually, the scientific scholarship publications are changing from paywall to GOA with authors paying the 408 charges (APC-GOA) for publication in biodiversity journals. Certainly, this is the best business model option 409 for the big publishers because it avoids losses generated by white (sometimes named black OA) or green 410 platforms such as Sci-Hub, ResearchGate, or Academia Inc. (site: academia.edu). Here it is important to 411 highlight that authors never received messages from Zootaxa demanding the removal of files from any such 412 platforms, quite unlike the crusade carried out by big publishers against these kinds of storage and access- 413 granting. 414 We are aware of the leading role that bibliometric indexes play in the science publishing industry, 415 as well as their considerable influence on how and where science is done nowadays. However, JIF cannot 416 determine the development of a whole scientific field, even when supporting agencies adopt it as a 417 criterion of quality. A high JIF does not necessarily come from a high-quality taxonomic study; it is probably 418 much more connected to the scope and diversity of methods and sources of data that are aligned to high 419 JIF ranked journals. Another aspect to be considered is that Zootaxa was focused initially on long papers on 420 descriptive taxonomy; subsequently, it gradually changed its scope and started accepting short notes and 421 studies on various subjects associated to zoological taxonomy/systematics. Curiously, soon after Clarivate 422 announced the reinstatement of the JIF of Zootaxa for next September (Clarivate 2020b), the journal’s 423 website refreshed its JIF, showing perhaps that it is willingly taking part in the JIF games. 424
425 Conclusions 426 Menaces to taxonomy as a science come from distinct sources and the low bibliometric values of its 427 journals is only one factor that contributes for establishing the so-called taxonomic impediment. Clarivate is 428 a for-profit company, but Magnolia Press Ltd. and other similar publishers are also not examples of 429 nonprofit NGOs. The reversion of the suppression of Zootaxa by Clarivate is irrelevant to biological sciences 430 and taxonomy because Journal Impact Factors are statistically illiterate (Curry 2012) and cause a great deal 431 of harm to science. This reinstatement should certainly not be regarded by taxonomists as a victory for the 432 13 field. As a community we should not endorse the villainy of bibliometric policies that bring more harm than 433 benefit to our field. 434 We hope the community of taxonomists gets engaged with renewed strength in actions directly 435 connected to the development and promotion of our science. Instead of being deeply focused on 436 proprietary gaming, irreproducible journal metrics sold to our institutions and research funders, controlled 437 by a USA/UK based company, itself acquired in 2016 by two private equity funds (Onex Corporation and 438 Baring Private Equity Asia – ONEX/BPEA; see Cision Ltd./PR Newswire 2016, https://prn.to/31nGDYC, BPEA 439 2019, https://bit.ly/3hm9yC4) we should perhaps concentrate, for instance, on securing professional 440 positions for young talented taxonomists, who are much needed for the proper development and 441 maintenance of museums, scientific collections, and public digital databases. We are sure that Zootaxa has 442 provided an invaluable service to the field of taxonomy. Suppression from JIF will not change or diminish 443 this remarkable contribution. 444 We emphasize that menace to taxonomy comes not much from the suppression of any specific 445 journal from a bibliometric platform belonging to a big company. Much more harm is caused by the limited 446 renewal of professional positions and the loss of collections, such as the huge ones that were housed at the 447 Museu Nacional of the Federal University of Rio de Janeiro. These are the real issues that should motivate 448 the engagement and action of taxonomists around the world. In short and loud, taxonomy is produced by 449 taxonomists, not by journals. We recognize the deep impact the JIF mania has on the careers of 450 taxonomists, due to governmental policies that embraced bibliometric evaluations in a highly competitive 451 environment, with researchers struggling for limited grants. However, our current challenges cannot be 452 dealt with through endorsement of the status quo. We need to change the focus. Also, it is contradictory to 453 argue in favor of the reinstatement of Zootaxa to the JIF without considering that this journal has this index 454 influenced by the currently high levels of self-citation. Regardless of its real significance, JIF is considered 455 one of the attractive qualities of Zootaxa. The bibliometrics game in science has its own rules. An honorable 456 choice would be to reject bibliometric indexes altogether, including JIF, instead of considering them when 457 convenient. We are seeing a bankruptcy of the system of scientific publications devoted to the knowledge 458 on biodiversity, at least for researchers, and it would be much better if the system could be somehow 459 reinvented with ways to support diamond open access (DOA) as its main goal. 460 Taxonomic groups that still need massive descriptive studies, with many species waiting to be 461 discovered, such as Coleoptera, Hymenoptera, Lepidoptera, Diptera, and Arachnida, have many journals 462 devoted specifically to them. The JIF of these journals is similar to that of Zootaxa and, of course, research 463 on those taxa can also be published in more general outlets in Entomology or Zoology categories. 464 Therefore, the high levels of self-citation in Zootaxa are hardly justifiable. It appears to us that these high 465 levels are caused by a sociological bias, being a side effect of the Zootaxa phenomenon. Myths about 466 Zootaxa as the unique journal that publishes taxonomic studies are clearly harmful to the field. In addition, 467 an urgent question must be answered: if Zootaxa decides to ignore JIF altogether, would it remain a good 468 14 vehicle for the publication of taxonomic papers? If your answer is no, there is certainly a big problem with 469 the community of practitioners in the taxonomic world. 470
